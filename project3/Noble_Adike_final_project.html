Understanding Preventable Crash Patterns in Washington, DC¶

Car crashes are not just random events. They often follow patterns based on location, time, road conditions, driver behavior, and other surrounding factors. For this project, I will be analyzing a dataset on car crashes in Washington, DC to better understand where and when crashes happen, and what factors may make them more likely to occur.

The dataset represents recorded crash incidents across DC and includes information that can help identify trends in crash frequency, location, timing, severity, and possible contributing conditions. The source of the dataset is DC’s public data portal, which makes it useful because it reflects real transportation and public safety data from the city.

By analyzing this data, I can look beyond individual incidents and focus on broader trends that may reveal risk areas, repeated conditions, or preventable factors connected to crashes in DC.

Objective and Research Question¶

The main objective of this project is to identify preventable patterns in car crash occurrences across Washington, DC. Instead of only looking at the total number of crashes, this analysis will focus on the conditions and trends that may explain why crashes happen more often in certain places, times, or situations.

Research Question¶

What are the preventable patterns seen in car crash occurrences in Washington, DC?

To answer this question, I will explore patterns such as:

  • Whether crashes happen more often in certain areas of DC
  • Whether crash frequency changes based on time of day, day of the week, or season
  • Whether specific crash types or conditions appear repeatedly
  • Whether certain factors are connected to more severe or frequent crashes

The goal is to use the data to better understand which crash patterns may be predictable and potentially preventable through safer driving, better road design, traffic enforcement, or public awareness.

Handling Missing Values¶

The missing values in this dataset need to be handled based on how useful each column is for the research question. Columns that are completely empty, or duplicated by better columns will be removed from the working dataset.

Important count columns, such as injuries, fatalities, speeding, impairment, pedestrians, bicyclists, and vehicle counts, will be filled with 0 because missing values in these fields usually mean no recorded count for that category.

For important location and text columns, missing values will be filled with "Unknown" so those records can still be included in the analysis. Rows missing the main crash date will be removed because they cannot be used for time-based crash pattern analysis.

INSIGHTS AND CONCLUSION¶

Research Question Answered Or Not?¶

My research question was answered because the analysis showed that car crashes in Washington, DC do follow preventable patterns instead of happening randomly. The clearest patterns appeared in crash timing, ward location, vulnerable road user involvement, and repeated high-risk periods. This means crash prevention efforts can be more targeted instead of treating every crash as an isolated event.

Most Significant Patterns and Trends¶

One major trend was that crashes were highest on Fridays, with 56,421 crashes, while Sundays had the lowest total. Crashes also increased over time and reached their highest yearly point in 2019 with 26,790 crashes before dropping afterward. Ward 2 consistently had the highest monthly crash counts, showing that some areas of DC experience more crash activity than others.

Key Insights from Visualizations¶

The preventable crash factor chart showed that pedestrian and bicycle involvement had the highest count, with 23,994 crashes, making vulnerable road users a major safety concern. The heatmap showed that crashes were most concentrated during evening hours, especially around 4 PM to 10 PM, with Friday and Tuesday evenings standing out. The intersection distance box plot showed that both speeding and non-speeding crashes often happened close to intersections, meaning intersection safety is important even when speeding is involved.

Limitations¶

One limitation is that this dataset only includes recorded crashes, so it may not fully represent crashes that were missing, delayed, or incorrectly reported. Some time values had to be cleaned because default or unknown crash times could affect time-based analysis. Also, the dataset does not fully explain why crashes happened because it does not include every possible factor, such as traffic volume, weather, road design, or driver behavior details.

Future Investigation¶

In the future, this analysis could be improved by adding traffic volume, weather, road type, and road design data to better explain why certain crash patterns happen. A deeper hotspot analysis could also help identify specific streets or intersections that need safety improvements. Another useful next step would be building a predictive model to estimate when and where crashes are most likely to occur.
